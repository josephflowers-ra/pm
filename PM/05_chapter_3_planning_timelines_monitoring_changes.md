## Chapter 3: Planning and Staying on Track - Timelines, Monitoring, and Adjusting

This is where your task list becomes a real plan. You will learn how to build a timeline, assign resources, track progress, and handle changes without losing momentum.

If Chapter 2 is about clarity, this chapter is about confidence. A simple, visible plan makes it easier to say yes to the right work and no to the wrong work.

If planning feels like a lot, start rough and improve as you learn. A plan is a working document, not a contract.

### Creating Timelines - Estimating Task Durations and Setting Milestones

#### Objective
Break your project into manageable segments, estimate task durations, and set milestones to track progress.
Why this matters: a timeline keeps the project from drifting and helps people trust the plan.

#### Steps to Create a Timeline
1. **Identify key tasks and deliverables.**
   - Use your WBS and focus on work tied to project goals.
   - Example: "Complete homepage design.".
2. **Estimate task durations.**
   - Use past data or team input.
   - Add buffer time for uncertainty.
3. **Set dependencies.**
   - Note which tasks must happen before others.
4. **Define milestones.**
   - Mark major progress points (draft complete, review done, launch).
5. **Build the timeline.**
   - Use a tool (Gantt, calendar, or spreadsheet).
6. **Review and adjust.**
   - Share with stakeholders and refine.
If your dates feel rough, revise after you see real progress.

#### Estimation Techniques: Four Ways to Guess Better
Every estimate is a guess. The techniques below just help you guess with less anxiety and more self-awareness. Pick the one that fits the type of work in front of you.

**1. Three-Point Estimation**
Useful when a task has real uncertainty and you want a single honest number.

- **Optimistic (O):** Everything goes well.
- **Most Likely (M):** Your best guess for a typical day.
- **Pessimistic (P):** Things go sideways.
- **Estimate:** `(O + 4M + P) / 6`

*Example:* Writing a homepage draft. Optimistic 2 days, Most Likely 4 days, Pessimistic 8 days. Estimate = (2 + 16 + 8) / 6 = **4.3 days**.

**2. T-Shirt Sizing**
Useful early in a project when precise numbers are false precision. Assign a size (XS, S, M, L, XL) to each task or deliverable. You are not committing to days yet. You are sorting work into rough buckets so the big stuff stands out.

- **XS:** A few hours.
- **S:** About a day.
- **M:** A few days.
- **L:** One to two weeks.
- **XL:** A month or more. Probably needs breaking down.

Convert to days later when the work is clearer.

**3. Analogous Estimation**
Useful when you have done something similar before. Instead of estimating from scratch, anchor on a past project.

*Example:* "Last volunteer event took 3 weeks to plan. This one is about the same size but has one more partner org, so let's plan for 4 weeks."

This is the fastest technique and often surprisingly accurate. Just be careful about what is actually comparable.

**4. Planning Poker (for teams)**
Useful when a group has shared context and estimates often diverge. Each team member picks a size privately (cards, fingers, or a quick chat app vote) and reveals at the same time. If estimates differ a lot, the outliers explain their thinking. You repeat until the team converges.

This is less about the number and more about surfacing what people know that others do not.

#### Estimation Rules of Thumb
- Break any task longer than two weeks into smaller pieces. Long estimates hide surprises.
- Add a buffer of 10–25% at the project level, not task by task. Task-level buffers compound and bloat the plan.
- Track your estimates versus actuals on a couple of recent projects. Your personal "bias factor" (e.g., "I usually under-estimate by 30%") is one of the most valuable planning tools you can develop.
- If a task is too unknown to estimate at all, plan a short "spike" (a time-boxed exploration) and estimate the real work afterward.

> **Pro tip: Track your bias factor.** Most people consistently lean one way. They over-estimate or under-estimate by a fairly stable percentage. You will not notice the pattern unless you write it down. Keep a tiny tally over a few projects:
>
> | Task | Estimated | Actual | Ratio (Actual ÷ Estimated) |
> | --- | --- | --- | --- |
> | Draft homepage copy | 4 days | 6 days | 1.5 |
> | Volunteer outreach | 5 days | 7 days | 1.4 |
> | Supply procurement | 3 days | 4 days | 1.3 |
>
> After five or ten rows, your average ratio is your bias factor. If it lives around 1.4, you under-estimate by 40%, so multiply your next gut-feel estimate by 1.4 before you publish it. This single number, kept truthfully, is worth more than any estimation framework.

#### Common Challenges and Solutions
- Inaccurate estimates: use three-point estimation, t-shirt sizing, or your personal bias factor.
- Overlapping tasks: map dependencies clearly.
- Scope creep: revisit the scope and communicate changes early.
A buffer is a simple way to protect your plan when the unexpected happens.

### Managing Resources - Assigning Roles and Balancing Budgets

#### Objective
Allocate people, tools, and budget so work stays balanced and affordable.
Why this matters: clear ownership and a basic budget prevent last-minute surprises.

#### Assigning Roles
- **Define tasks** using your WBS.
- **Match skills** to tasks.
- **Clarify expectations** with a RACI chart.
- **Adjust** if workloads shift.
If you are unsure who should own something, pick a reasonable person and confirm later.

#### Balancing Budgets
- Start with realistic estimates.
- Track spending regularly.
- Focus spending on high-impact items.
- Reserve 10-15% for contingency.
Even a rough budget helps you make better decisions.

### Monitoring Progress - Using Simple Metrics

#### Objective
Track progress so you can spot issues early and adjust quickly.
Why this matters: small issues are easier to fix when you catch them early.

#### Why It Matters
Monitoring prevents surprises and keeps stakeholders informed.

#### Simple Metrics That Work
- Task completion rate.
- Budget used vs. planned.
- Schedule variance (ahead/on track/behind).
- Team utilization when relevant.

#### Steps to Implement Tracking
1. Choose 2-3 metrics tied to your goals.
2. Set up a tracking system (sheet, tool, or board).
3. Update on a regular schedule.
4. Watch for trends, not just snapshots.
5. Share updates with stakeholders.
Pick the simplest metrics that tell you the truth.

### Handling Changes - Strategies to Adapt

#### Objective
Manage change without losing control of scope, timeline, or budget.
Why this matters: unmanaged change is stressful and slows the project.

#### Why Change Happens
- Stakeholder needs evolve.
- Budgets shift.
- Resources change.
- External factors disrupt plans.

#### A Simple Change Process
1. **Anticipate changes early.**
   - Identify likely change areas during planning.
2. **Assess impact.**
   - How does this affect scope, timeline, and budget?
3. **Decide: accept, modify, or reject.**
   - Adjust plans or phase the request.
4. **Communicate clearly.**
   - Confirm decisions and update documentation.
5. **Track changes in a change log.**
If you are unsure whether to accept a change, ask what it replaces or delays.

#### Examples
1. **Team member leaves:** Reassign tasks and adjust deadlines.
2. **Client requests new features:** Assess impact and negotiate priorities.
3. **Vendor delay:** Find alternatives and shift milestones.

### Reflection Prompts
Take a moment to stress-test your plan.
1. If one thing slips by a week, what is the knock-on effect on the rest of the timeline?
2. Where did you add buffer time, and is it visible in the plan, or hiding inside task estimates?
3. Which metric would you actually check next week to know if the project is on track?

### Chapter 3 Checklist
Use this checklist to guide your next update.
- Timeline includes realistic durations and dependencies.
- Milestones are defined and visible.
- Roles and budget are assigned and tracked.
- Progress metrics are updated regularly.
- Changes are evaluated and documented.
- Change decisions are communicated and logged.

### Chapter 3 Cheat Sheet
A fast reference for estimation, tracking, and changes.

**Big ideas**

- **Milestone:** a visible progress marker, not a task. Drawn as a point on the timeline.
- **Three-point estimate:** `(O + 4M + P) / 6`. An honest blend of best, typical, and worst case.
- **T-shirt sizing:** XS, S, M, L, XL. Rough buckets when days are false precision.
- **Buffer:** extra time added at the project level to absorb surprises.
- **Change log:** a running record of what shifted and why.

**Do this**

- Estimate with a named technique, not gut feel alone.
- Add buffer at the project level (10–25%), not hidden inside each task.
- Pick 2–3 metrics tied to the goal and update them on a cadence.
- Log every accepted or rejected change with the impact.

**Avoid this**

- Padding every task "just in case." Buffers compound and bloat the plan.
- Tracking ten metrics nobody reads.
- Accepting changes without an impact check.

**Try it**

Sketch a one-page Gantt strip for your project and mark one milestone with a diamond.
